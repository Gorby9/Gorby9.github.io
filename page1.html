About PLS

Corticospinal Tract

Primary Lateral Sclerosis is an upper motor neuron disease that affects voluntary muscle movement.

Primary because the upper motor neurons connect the Primary Motor Cortex in the brain to the spinal cord.

Lateral, meaning 'away from the midline'. The neurons travel down the cortical tracts on either side of the brain midline.

Sclerosis, meaning 'stiffening'. The neurons malfunction, and eventually die off.

PLS causes spasticity, hyper reflexes, and muscle weakness. There is usually mild muscle atrophy due to disuse. It affects basic activities of daily living, including walking, use of arms, core strength, fine motor control, speech, and swallowing. It can also cause pseudobulbar affect (PBA), which means that the person exhibits inappropriate laughing or crying. Many people with PLS experience bladder urgency.

Spastic Paraplegia Foundation

PLS is a rare disease, so there aren't any national or international organisations that specialize in PLS research and support. Instead PLS often comes under the umbrella of motor neuron or ALS charities. For patient support, try Team Gleason or the ALS Association. In Canada there is the ALS Society and in the UK, the Motor Neuron Disease Association. Information about PLS can also be found at the Spastic Paraplegia Foundation website.
