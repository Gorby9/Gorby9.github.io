About PLS

Corticospinal Tract

Primary Lateral Sclerosis is an upper motor neuron disease that affects voluntary muscle movement.

Primary because the upper motor neurons connect the Primary Motor Cortex in the brain to the spinal cord.

Lateral, meaning 'away from the midline'. The neurons travel down the cortical tracts on either side of the brain midline.

Sclerosis, meaning 'stiffening'. The neurons malfunction, and eventually die off.

PLS causes spasticity, hyperreflexia, and muscle weakness. There is often mild muscle atrophy due to disuse. It impacts basic activities of daily living, such as walking, arm movement, core strength, fine motor control, speech, and swallowing. PLS can also result in pseudobulbar affect (PBA), causing inappropriate laughing or crying. Many individuals with PLS also experience bladder urgency.

Spastic Paraplegia Foundation

Since PLS is a rare disease, there are no national or international organizations specifically dedicated to PLS research and support. Instead, PLS is often grouped under the umbrella of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) charities. For patient support, you can reach out to organizations like Team Gleason or the ALS Association. In Canada there is the ALS Society, and in the UK, the Motor Neuron Disease Association. Additonally, information about PLS can be found on the Spastic Paraplegia Foundation website.
