Molecular and Cellular Mechanisms Affected in ALS

PLS research is limited by the rarity of the disease, variety of patient phenotypes, and lack of biomarkers. Our best hope is that advancements in understanding and treating amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), may eventually benefit PLS as well. In fact, researchers now believe that ALS may represent distinct diseases each with different pathways leading to motor neuron degeneration. For information about current ALS research visit: ALS TDI, Answer ALS, NEALS, or Target ALS.

Primary Lateral Sclerosis Natural History Study

A 30-site, 24-month study, funded by multiple sources, is currently underway to measure clinical and biological data from fifty PLS patients. The study divides participants into two groups: early-stage (less than 4 years) and well-established (4-15 years) cases of PLS. The goal is to gain a clearer understanding of the natural progression of the disease, which could help facilitate PLS clinical trials in the future.
