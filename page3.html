Tips

Hi, my name is Lori, and I compiled the content on this website. My PLS symptoms began in 2011, starting with speech difficulties and PBA. I currently use a rollator to get around the house and a wheelchair for outings. It takes me awhile to eat, but I haven't had to modify my diet. My speech is understandable about two-thirds of the time. While medications help to reduce some symptoms, I have yet to find anything that slows progression. PLS has spread to all of the muscles in my body, but I am thankful that I haven't completely lost function anywhere.

People with PLS are at a high risk of falling because their muscles don't receive proper signals from the brain. These falls tend to be severe, leading to bruises, cuts, broken bones and sometimes head injuries. While doctors usually advise trying to stay loose during a fall, the tight muscles associated with PLS makes this difficult, especially because with PLS the tightness involuntarily increases during a fall. Additionally, many people with PLS have a heightened startle response that can trigger falling more often.

Using mobility aids, like walkers or wheelchairs, is important when you have PLS. Some people consider using aids as "giving up", fearing that it will lead to dependence and reduced mobility. I see it differently. Aids allow me to move more freely and often because I can do so safely. They also help me maintain some independence and stay active in my community. I use a variety of devices, including a walker, a 2-in-1 rollator (which converts to a transport chair), a portable wheelchair, and a full-featured power wheelchair. I'm fortunate to have received some of this equipment from the ALS Society of BC.
